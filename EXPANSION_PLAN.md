# SAT Studio — Question Set Expansion Plan

## Executive Summary

Tommy's SAT Studio currently has **34 practice sets** with ~350 questions across Math and Reading & Writing domains. This plan identifies critical gaps and proposes 5 high-priority new sets to fill them.

## Strategic Gaps & Why They Matter

### 1. **Hard & Insane Difficulty Tiers (Math)**
- **SAT reality**: The last 5–7 questions in each module jump in difficulty; scoring 750+ requires consistent Hard/Expert performance.
- **Studio gap**: All advanced math tops out at "Difícil." No 2-step algebra + functions mashups, no nested function composition, no parametric systems.
- **Impact**: Tommy practices Difícil repeatedly but hits a wall at real exam's top difficulty.

### 2. **Cross-Domain Mashups (Math)**
- **SAT reality**: Late-module questions combine multiple skills (e.g., solve a quadratic disguised as a system, or interpret exponential data with algebra).
- **Studio gap**: Sets are siloed by domain (Algebra, Adv Math, Data, Geo). Single-domain "hard" is not the same as multi-domain hard.
- **Impact**: Tommy solves each domain separately but struggles when combining them under time pressure.

### 3. **Limited SPR (Student-Produced Response) Coverage**
- **SAT reality**: Real SAT has ~13 SPR (grid-in) across both modules. Current studio has only 3.
- **Studio gap**: Only scattered throughout; no dedicated 10–12 question SPR drill for accuracy and entry-method practice.
- **Impact**: Tommy under-practices grid-in mechanics, leading to careless errors on test day.

### 4. **Vocabulary Nuance (R&W)**
- **SAT reality**: Word-choice questions test subtle distinctions (e.g., "sparing" vs. "insincere" praise; "calibrated" vs. "random" systems). These are score-setters for 650–750+ students.
- **Studio gap**: rw-set3 (Easy) → rw-wic2 (Media) → rw-set4 (Hard) jumps. No "Medium-Hard bridge" with tricky second-choice traps.
- **Impact**: Tommy passes easy vocab but trips on Medium-Hard; needs drills on the nuance-heavy zone.

### 5. **Paired Passages & Dual Perspectives**
- **SAT reality**: 1 paired-passage set = 10–12 questions testing comparison, contrast, tone shifts, and synthesis across two texts.
- **Studio gap**: Studio has no paired-passage set at all.
- **Impact**: Tommy has never timed a "compare Author A's skepticism vs. Author B's enthusiasm" question; a common trap.

### 6. **Author's Attitude & Tone**
- **SAT reality**: "Author's tone is best described as…" and "Author uses ___ to suggest…" appear in 2–4 questions per module.
- **Studio gap**: No dedicated tone/attitude drill; only buried in broader "Ideas" sets.
- **Impact**: Tommy confuses "skeptical yet curious" with "dismissive"; tone shifts are high-error zones.

### 7. **Form, Structure & Sense (R&W Grammar)**
- **SAT reality**: Verb tenses, parallel structure, logical modifiers account for ~15–18 Conventions questions.
- **Studio gap**: Sets focus on punctuation + agreement. Verb forms (past perfect, gerunds, infinitives) underrepresented.
- **Impact**: Tommy aces "The children is running" but misses "Having completed the task, she…" (misplaced modifier).

## Recommended New Question Sets (Priority Order)

### **PRIORITY 1: Math — Hard Algebra + Functions Mashup (NEW)**
**Why now:** Tommy aces Algebra and Adv Math separately but struggles when they collide in Module 2.

**Set Properties:**
- **ID:** `math-mashup-alg-func`
- **Title:** Math — Challenging Algebra + Functions (Mashup)
- **Difficulty:** **Hard** (4–5 level up from current Difícil)
- **Domain:** Algebra + Advanced Math + Problem-Solving
- **Q Count:** 12 (10 MC + 2 SPR)
- **Time Est.:** 14–16 min

**Rationale:**
- Late-module SAT questions nest skills: "Solve for x in this quadratic within a system," "Find the range of this exponential function given a constraint," "This data fits f(x) = a·2^(bx); find a and b."
- Studio currently has no single set where domainslur together under time pressure.
- **Focus areas:**
  - Systems with quadratics (substitute to eliminate, recognize parabola + line intersections)
  - Piecewise functions + inequalities (determine the domain of f when f(x) > g(x))
  - Exponential models with constraints (e.g., initial value + doubling time → solve for t)
  - Parametric insight: Solve for a parameter that changes behavior (amplitude, phase, decay rate)

**Example Question Stem (Conceptual):**
> "A company's profit, in dollars, is modeled by P(n) = −2n² + 80n − 600, where n is the number of units sold. For which positive values of n is the profit exactly $0? (Answer: solve −2n² + 80n − 600 = 0 using quadratic formula or factoring. Result: n = 15 or n = 20. The profit is zero at 15 and 20 units—break-even points.)"

**Expected student error:**
- Forgets to factor out −2 before factoring (−2(n² − 40n + 300) = 0 → n² − 40n + 300 = 0)
- Confuses "profit = 0" with "loss" and picks negative n
- Enters answer as "15, 20" instead of recognizing two grid-in entries

### **PRIORITY 2: Reading & Writing — Paired Passages: Tone & Perspective Shift (NEW)**
**Why now:** Paired passages are ~10% of real SAT R&W; studio has zero.

**Set Properties:**
- **ID:** `rw-paired-tone`
- **Title:** R&W — Paired Passages: Contrast & Tone Shifts
- **Difficulty:** **Hard** (Real paired passages are upper-medium to Hard)
- **Domain:** Ideas + Craft & Structure
- **Q Count:** 12 (all MC, 2 passages with 6 Qs each)
- **Time Est.:** 12–14 min

**Rationale:**
- Paired passages on real SAT often feature:
  1. **Passage A:** Scientific/skeptical tone; Passage B: Advocacy/optimistic tone (or vice versa)
  2. Questions ask "How does Author B's perspective challenge Author A's claim?" or "Both authors agree that ___, but differ on ___."
  3. Traps: Student picks a detail from A that's true but doesn't fit the question about B's view.
- Studio has no practice for this scenario.
- **Focus areas:**
  - Identifying central claims in each passage under time pressure
  - Nuancing tone ("concerned but practical" vs. "dismissive")
  - Synthesis: "Which statement would Author B add to Author A's argument?"
  - Inference: "Author B likely disagrees with Author A's claim that ___."

**Example Setup (Conceptual):**
> **Passage A (Historical perspective):** Museum curator argues that AI-generated art lacks the human struggle and emotion of hand-made pieces. "A computer cannot suffer; therefore, it cannot create."
> **Passage B (Technologist perspective):** AI researcher counters that emotion ≠ artistic merit. "Bach's fugues follow mathematical rules; does that diminish their beauty?"
>
> **Question:** "How would Author B likely respond to Author A's statement 'A computer cannot suffer'?"
> - A) By pointing out that suffering is not necessary for artistic achievement
> - B) By suggesting that computers will soon be able to suffer
> - C) By acknowledging that this is a disadvantage of AI
> - D) By arguing that mathematics is a form of suffering

**Expected student error:**
- Picks B (plausible but Author B never says that)
- Misreads Author A's position as "computers should suffer" instead of "lack of suffering limits art"

### **PRIORITY 3: Math — Grid-In SPR Focused Practice (NEW)**
**Why now:** Tommy has only 3 SPR questions across all math sets; real SAT has 13.

**Set Properties:**
- **ID:** `math-spr-drill`
- **Title:** Math — Student-Produced Response (SPR) Drill
- **Difficulty:** **Media-Hard mix** (6 Media, 6 Difícil to build progression)
- **Domain:** All math domains
- **Q Count:** 12 (all SPR/grid-in)
- **Time Est.:** 16–18 min (accounts for careful entry)

**Rationale:**
- SPR questions require numeric answers entered on a grid (no multiple choice = higher error rate if mechanics are weak).
- Entry traps: decimals (0.5 vs. 5, leading zeros), fractions (entry order), negatives (not allowed on real SAT).
- Studio should drill SPR mechanics separately so Tommy internalizes:
  - Filling left-to-right, no commas in big numbers
  - Decimals occupy one column
  - If answer > 9,999, express as decimal (e.g., 15,000 → can't enter; rewrite as 1.5 × 10^4 or see if a simplified form exists)
  - Negative answers not allowed on real SAT
- **Focus areas:**
  - Single-step algebra (solve for x, evaluate at a point)
  - Systems → solve for one variable → grid-in
  - Ratios (e.g., "ratio of A to B"; enter as decimal, e.g., 2.5)
  - Percentages & probability (e.g., 0.35 or 35%, which should enter as 0.35)
  - Data: mean, median, range

**Example Question Stem (Conceptual):**
> "If 3x + 5 = 2(x + 4), what is the value of x? (Grid in your answer.)"
> Answer: 3. (Common errors: enter 3.0 [wastes bubbles]; enter −3 [wrong sign])

### **PRIORITY 4: Reading & Writing — Author's Attitude & Nuanced Tone Drill (NEW)**
**Why now:** Tone questions are +3% of R&W, but studio has no dedicated tone-focused set.

**Set Properties:**
- **ID:** `rw-tone-attitude`
- **Title:** R&W — Author's Attitude & Tone (Advanced)
- **Difficulty:** **Media-Hard** (tone is easier to teach than punctuation, but nuance is hard)
- **Domain:** Craft & Structure
- **Q Count:** 10 (all MC, fill-the-blank passage-based)
- **Time Est.:** 10–12 min

**Rationale:**
- Tone questions ask for author's attitude toward a subject, tone of a phrase, or rhetorical effect of a word choice.
- Real SAT tone traps:
  1. **Confusing similar tones:** "Skeptical" vs. "dismissive" vs. "concerned" (all negative, but different nuances)
  2. **Irony/sarcasm:** Author says X but means ¬X (e.g., "Surely this is a great idea" = sarcasm)
  3. **Tone shifts:** Passage starts formal, ends casual (or vice versa); question asks about the *whole* tone, not just the end.
- **Focus areas:**
  - Identifying tone markers (word choice, sentence structure, punctuation)
  - Distinguishing related tones (ironic vs. mocking; speculative vs. uncertain; resigned vs. accepting)
  - Tone within a larger argument (Author presents skeptical evidence, then advocates → tone is cautiously optimistic, not purely skeptical)

**Example Question Stem (Conceptual):**
> "As used in the text, the author's tone toward the proposed policy can best be described as:
> - A) Enthusiastic and unqualified support
> - B) Cautious support with reservations
> - C) Indifferent neutrality
> - D) Outright hostility"
> 
> (Passage excerpt: "While the policy has merit, the implementation timeline fails to account for existing infrastructure constraints—a critical oversight.")
> Answer: B. (Common error: Pick C if student doesn't notice "has merit"; pick D if they overweight "critical oversight.")

### **PRIORITY 5: Reading & Writing — Vocabulary: Medium-Hard Bridge (NEW)**
**Why now:** Gap between Easy (rw-set3) and Hard (rw-set4); students need scaffolding.

**Set Properties:**
- **ID:** `rw-vocab-bridge`
- **Title:** R&W — Words in Context: Medium-Hard Bridge
- **Difficulty:** **Hard** (but with scaffolding; transitions from Media to Difícil)
- **Domain:** Craft & Structure
- **Q Count:** 12 (all MC, meaning-in-context + fill-in-blank)
- **Time Est.:** 12–14 min

**Rationale:**
- Real SAT vocabulary questions at "Hard" level test:
  1. **Multiple-meaning words** (e.g., "run" can mean operate, sprint, stocking, or a series)
  2. **Context clues requiring inference** (word not defined explicitly; must infer from surrounding ideas)
  3. **Nuanced synonyms** (e.g., "spare" [restrained] vs. "sparse" [thin])
- Studio jumps Easy → Hard; medium-difficulty vocab helps Tommy build intuition before the hard level.
- **Focus areas:**
  - Vocabulary from academic contexts (not just literary)
  - Multiple-meaning words with context clues
  - Synonym distinctions (e.g., "stubborn" vs. "resolute" vs. "obstinate")
  - Negative context clues ("Though X was described as ___, the evidence showed…")

**Example Question Stem (Conceptual):**
> "Which choice completes the text with the most logical and precise word or phrase?
> 
> The politician's public statements were deliberately %BLANK%: she spoke in generalities and avoided any specific policy proposals.
> 
> - A) meticulous
> - B) vague
> - C) succinct
> - D) passionate"
> 
> Answer: B. (Common error: Pick C [succinct sounds similar to vague]; A and D are traps [meticulous = detailed, passion ≠ vagueness].)
